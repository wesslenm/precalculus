    Section 2.1 Exponential Functions, Definitions and Graphs

    Objectives

    • Recognize an exponential function and distinguish it from a polynomial / power function
      🔗
      🔗
    • Identify if an exponential function is modelling exponential growth or exponential decay based on its graph and/or equation
      🔗
      🔗
    • Identify fundamental characteristics of exponential functions.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Compare and contrast characteristics of exponential functions with those of polynomial functions.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Describe transformations of exponential functions.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Describe how transformations of exponential functions change the properties and characteristics (such as the horizontal asymptote and end behaviours) of the transformed function.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Define the number \(e\) and the natural exponential function \(y=e^x\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Where will this be used in calculus?

    • Exponential functions are an important family of functions that are used throughout calculus to illustrate calculus related concepts and to practice computations.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Exponential functions are solutions to many types of differential equations that model the rate of changing quantities such as population growth, the spread of infectious diseases, and the cooling of a liquid.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Exponential functions are used to model the growth of an investment due to compound interest.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    In Section 1.2, we reviewed some fundamental exponent laws and learned how to re-write mathematical expressions involving exponents. While this gives us a strong understanding of exponential calculations, we will now start looking at properties and characteristics of the exponential function, which is often used in mathematical models to describe rapid growth or decay. A defining characteristic of exponential functions is that they change at a rate that is directly proportional to the amount of the quantity that is currently present.
    🔗

    Subsection 2.1.1 What is an Exponential Function?

    An exponential function is any function in the form \(f(x)=b^x,\) where \(b>0\) and \(b \neq 1\text{.}\) Here, we call the parameter \(b\) the base of the function, which determines the type of exponential relationship that is being modelled:

    Subsection 2.1.2 Euler’s Number and The Natural Exponential Function

    While exponential functions can have infinitely many bases, there is one special exponential function that we will deal with almost exclusively in our calculus classes: The Natural Exponential Function.
    🔗

    Aside
    Study tip:
    🔗
    Euler’s Number \(e\) is named after Leonhard Euler (1707-1783). He also introduced the notation \(f(x)\) for functions. Read more about the remarkable mathematian Leonhard Euler here.
    🔗

    The Natural Exponential Function.

    The Natural Exponential Function is an exponential function whose base is the special number denoted \(e\text{,}\) called Euler’s Number, which is an irrational constant given by \(e \approx 2.7182818 \ldots\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Aside
    Study tip:
    🔗
    You should know (memorize) a few things about the number \(e\text{.}\) You are expected to know that \(e\) is irrational, and \(e \approx 2.7\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Why the number \(e\text{?}\)
    🔗
    The base \(e\) is important because with this base, the slope of the exponential function at \(x=0\) is exactly 1. That might not sound important now, but this means that when differentiating (which we will do a lot in calculus!) this means that the derivative of \(f(x)=e^x\) is simpler than the derivative of any other exponential function.
    🔗
    Later (using calculus) it will be possible to prove that the number \(e\) (with the special slope as above) is also given by
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{n \to \infty} \left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^n \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The natural exponential function, \(y=e^x\text{,}\) can be approximated by \(y=2.718^x\text{,}\) which can be thought of as simply an exponential function that grows faster than \(y=2^x\) but not quite as fast as \(y=3^x.\) The graph of \(y=e^x\) is shown in the diagram below along with the graphs of \(y=2^x\) and \(y=3^x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    As we can see, \(y=e^x\) is characteristically similar to any other exponential function, and should not be thought of as a fundamentally "different" function. For our purposes, we should think of the natural exponential function as simply being an exponential function with a specific base, \(e\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Subsection 2.1.3 Graphs of Exponential Functions

    Just like any other type of parent function, exponential functions have a set of "default" characteristics. Some of these characteristics may change based on the transformations of the function, but the standard exponential function \(f(x)=e^x\) has the following characteristics:
    🔗
    Domain: \(x \in \mathbb{R}\)
    Range: \(y \gt 0\)
    Horizontal asymptote: \(y=0\)
    Vertical asymptote: None
    \(x\)-intercept: None
    \(y\)-intercept: \((0,1)\)

    Subsection 2.1.4 Exponential Rules

    We saw several exponential rules in Section 1.2 and they were summarized in Assemblage . We write the first three again here for easy reference, and this time we write them in terms of \(e^x\) because we will often need to use them in that format.
    🔗

    Exponential Rules in terms of \(e^x\).

    The following are true for all positive numbers \(x\) and \(y\text{,}\) and any real numbers \(a\) and \(b\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation} e^xe^y=e^{x+y}\tag{2.1.1} \end{equation}
    \begin{equation} e^{x-y} = \frac{e^x}{e^y}\tag{2.1.2} \end{equation}
    \begin{equation} (e^x)^y = e^{xy}\tag{2.1.3} \end{equation}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
