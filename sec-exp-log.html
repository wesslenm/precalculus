    Section 2.2 Logarithmic Functions, Definitions and Graphs

    Objectives

    • Identify a logarithm as the inverse of an exponential function.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Convert between exponential and logarithmic forms.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Solve problems involving the natural logarithmic function, \(y=\ln x\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Identify graphs of logarithmic functions, and their properties.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Where will this be used in calculus?

    Logarithms are used nearly as often as exponential functions in calculus.
    🔗
    • Logarithmic functions are used as important examples of functions, for practicing and illustrating calculus concepts.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Logarithmic functions are used in the differentiation technique Logarithmic Differentiation.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Logarithmic functions are essential when integrating rational functions.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    As we know, many functions have an inverse function, which "undoes" the work done by the function. An inverse function reverses the inputs and outputs of the original function. As we will review in this section, a logarithm is simply the inverse of an exponential function. In this section, we will review basic properties and characteristics of logarithmic calculations, and explore their algebraic relationship to exponentiation.
    🔗

    Subsection 2.2.1 Definitions of Logarithms

    First we need a reminder regarding inverse functions:
    🔗
    A function \(y=f(x)\) has an inverse if and only if \(y=f(x)\) is one-to-one. An equavalent way of saying this is that a function \(y=f(x)\) has an inverse if and only if the graph of \(y=f(x)\) passes the horizontal line test (HLT). A function "passes the Horizontal Line tests" if no horizontal line crosses the curve more than once).
    🔗
    If we look at graphs of a few exponential functions (for example \(y=e^x\) and \(y=2^x\) and \(y=3^x\)), we see that they pass the HLT. They therefore have inverses. The inverse of each equponential function, is also a function. We call those inverfunctions logarithmic functions.
    🔗

    Aside
    Study tip:
    🔗
    For exponential functions we required \(b \gt 0\) and \(b \neq 1\) so we will do the same for logarithmic functions, since logarithms are based on expoential functions.
    🔗

    Definition of logarithmic functions.

    For any positive number \(b\not= 1\text{,}\) the logarithmic function with base \(b\text{,}\) written \(\log_b \ x\) is defined by:
    \begin{equation*} y = \log_b \ x \mbox{ if and only if } x = b^y \end{equation*}
    In other words, \(y=\log_b \ x\) is the inverse function of \(y = b^x\text{.}\) Note that \(b \gt 0\) and \(b \neq 1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    In a similar way that the expression \(\sqrt{x}\) is read as "the square root of \(x\text{,}\)" the expression \(\log_b \ x\) is read as "log, base \(b\text{,}\) of \(x\text{.}\)" In general, logarithmic expressions can be read in the following ways:
    🔗

    Aside
    Study tip:
    🔗
    \(y=\log_b x\) is a function. It requires an input. It would be complete non-sense to write \(\sqrt{ \hspace{3mm} }\text{,}\) without any number or expression to take the square root of. Similarly it would be complete non-sense to write \(y=\log_b\) , without any number or expression to take log of.
    🔗
    Expression: Read as:
    \(\log_b \ x\) "log, base \(b\text{,}\) of \(x\)"
    \(\log_3(5)\) "log, base \(3\text{,}\) of \(5\)"
    \(\log_2(8)=3\) "log, base \(2\text{,}\) of \(8\) equals \(3\)"

    Subsection 2.2.2 Exponential and Logarithmic Forms

    When we have an exponential expression, we can re-write the expression as an equivalent logarithmic expression by observing the relationship between the expression’s input and output.
    🔗
    One useful way to conceptualize this is to compare a logarithmic calculation to a radical calculation:
    🔗
    Power Form: Radical Form:
    \(x^{\textcolor{red}{n}}=y\) \(\textcolor{red}{\sqrt[n]{\textcolor{black}{y}}}=x\)
    \(5^{\textcolor{red}{3}}=125\) \(\textcolor{red}{\sqrt[3]{\textcolor{black}{125}}}=5\)
    \(2^{\textcolor{red}{5}}=32\) \(\textcolor{red}{\sqrt[5]{\textcolor{black}{32}}}=2\)
    Each pair of statements are equivalent to each other. This feels intuitive since we have grown an understanding that radical expressions (like cube roots) are the inverse operations to powers, thus every power of degree \(n\) has a corresponding radical inverse operation (the \(n\)^{th} root) that "cancels out" the power.
    🔗
    A similar line of reasoning can be applied to exponential expressions; each exponential operation has a corresponding logarithmic inverse operation that "undoes" its work. In the same way that we can switch between power form and radical form, we can switch between Exponential Form and Logarithmic Form:
    🔗
    Exponential Form: Logarithmic Form:
    \(\textcolor{red}{b}^x=y\) \(\log_{\textcolor{red}{b}}y=x\)"
    \(\textcolor{red}{5}^3=125\) \(\log_{\textcolor{red}{5}}(125)=3\)"
    \(\textcolor{red}{2}^5=32\) \(\log_{\textcolor{red}{2}}(32)=5\)"
    Note that when re-writing an exponential expression in its equivalent logarithmic form, the logarithm retains the same base as the exponential function, and the input and output are swapped.
    🔗

    Example 2.16. Write in logarithmic form.

    Write the expression \(6^{x}=36\) in logarithmic form.
    🔗
    Answer.
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{x = \log_{6} (36)} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    \(6^{x}=36\) is equivalent to writing \(x = \log_{6} (36)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Notice that the base is \(6\) for both the logarithm and the exponential.
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{x = \log_{6} (36)} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Aside
    Study tip:
    🔗
    Think of this as \(6^{(...)}\) on one side of the equal sign is equivalent to \(log_{6} (...)\) on the other side of the equal sign. Notice that the base is \(6\) for both the logarithm and the exponential.
    🔗

    Subsection 2.2.3 The Natural Logarithm \(\ln x\)

    As we saw above, each exponential expression and function has a corresponding logarithmic inverse. In Section 2.1, we discussed the natural exponential function, \(y=e^x\text{,}\) as the most important exponential function in calculus. The inverse of that function is the most important logarithmic function, and is written \(y=\ln x\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Definition of \(\ln x\).

    The natural logarithm is defined as the inverse of the natural exponential function \(y=e^x\text{.}\) The natural logrithm is denoted by \(\ln x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Equivalently, the natural logarithm, \(\ln x\text{,}\) is simply the logarithm with a base of \(e\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The mathematical notation \(\ln (...)\) is pronounced like "lawn" and the function \(y=\ln x\) can be read as "\(y\) equals `lawn’ of \(x\text{.}\)"
    🔗
    From the definition it follows that:
    \begin{equation*} y = \ln x \quad \mbox{ is the same as } \quad y = \log_e x \end{equation*}
    and because \(y=\ln x\) is the inverse of \(y=e^x\) it is true that:
    \begin{equation*} y = \ln x \quad \mbox{ if and only if } \quad x = e^y \end{equation*}
    Also, \(y=e^x\) is the inverse of \(y=\ln x\text{.}\) (The two functions are inverses of each other.)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.20. Write in exponential form.

    Write the expression \(\ln(x+6)=4\) in its equivalent exponential form.
    🔗
    Answer.
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{x+6 = e^4} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    \(\ln(x+6)=4\) is equivalent to writing \(x+6 = e^4\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Notice that the base is \(e\) for both the logarithm and the exponential.
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{x+6 = e^4} \end{equation*}
    (Answering with \(x= e^4-6\) would also be OK here.)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.2.4 Graphs of Logarithmic Functions

    In this part, we will examine logarithms from a graphical approach by examining the key characteristics and properties of the graphs of logarithmic functions, and compare these properties to those of the graphs of exponential functions.
    🔗
    For our purposes in calculus, we will limit this part to only include the natural logarithm, \(y=\ln x\text{,}\) even though much of what is discussed here applies to any logarithm.
    🔗
    As seen earlier, \(y=\ln x\) is the inverse of the function \(y=e^x\text{.}\) To obtain the graph of an inverse function from the original graph, we reflect the graph in the diagonal line \(y=x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Because the natural logarithmic function is the inverse of the natural exponential function, their graphs are related to each other by a relection in the diagonal. Since inverting a function switches its inputs and outputs, many of the characteristics of functions and their inverses have many commonalities. Let’s compare some properties of exponential and logarithmic functions:
    🔗

    Aside
    Study tip:
    🔗
    Notice how all the details in the table of characteristics stem from switching \(x\) and \(y\) values (which what happens when you find an inverse).
    🔗
    Characteristic: Exponential Function: Logarithmic Function:
    Domain: \(x \in \mathbb{R}\) \(x \gt 0\)
    Range: \(y \gt 0\) \(x \in \mathbb{R}\)
    Horizontal asymptote: \(y=0\) None
    Vertical asymptote: None \(x=0\)
    \(x\)-intercept: \((1,0)\) None
    \(y\)-intercept: \((0,1)\) None
    Of course, graphs of logarithms can also be transformed (translated, stretched, compressed and reflected) just like any other type of graph.
    🔗
