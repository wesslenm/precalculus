    Section 3.1 Trigonometric definitions and basic facts

    Objectives

    • Be familiar with radian measure of angles, and standard notation related to angles.
    • Use the right angled triangle definitions and the general definitions of the 6 trigonometric functions, and interchange between the two.
    • Be familiar with basic relationships between trigonometric functions.
    • Evaluate the 6 trigonometric functions without a calculator, for common angles such as those listed in Common angles.

    Where will this be used in calculus?

    Trigonometric functions are used extensively throughout calculus. Being able to work with, and evaluate, trigonometric functions is essential in many applications. Here are a few specific examples:
    • Trigonometric functions are used in many modeling contexts, for example when modeling wave motions.
    • A technique called trigonometric substitution allows us to solve integration problems such as for example \(\int \sqrt{4-x^2} \ dx\text{.}\)
    • Optimization problems sometimes involve trigonometric relationships. For example, maximizing the area of a triangle given certain side lengths requires an understanding of trigonometric functions.
    In this section, we will work with trigonometric functions. We will examine relationships between the 6 trigonometric functions and will discuss how to evaluate them for commonly used inputs.

    Subsection 3.1.1 Angle notation

    In calculus, we will mostly use radians to measure angles. This is because several formulas and techniques in calculus are much easier to work with when using radians (compared to degrees). If you are more familiar with angles measured in degrees, you may want to start by using this conversion chart. However, it is useful to develop a feel for radian measures (rather than converting to degrees each time).
    Study tip:
    Use radians as much as possible and try to develop a feel for their size. Try to develop a feel for how large for example \(\frac{\pi}{3}\) radians is, withough needing to convert it to degrees. Use degrees as little as possible (or not at all) when you study calculus. If you use a calculator to check your answers, make sure it is set to radian mode.
    An angle placed on the coordinate system is in standard position when its vertex is at the origin and its initial side is on the positive \(x\)-axis. See Figure 3.2. A positive angle is in the counterclockwise direction. A negative angle is in the clockwise direction.
    The unit circle can be used to define trigonometric functions. The unit circle is a circle with centre at the origin, and radius 1, as shown in Figure 3.3. The point \(P(x,y)\) in Figure 3.3 is the terminal point. The terminal point is defined as the point where the terminal side of an angle (in standard position) intersects the unit circle. The \(x\) and \(y\) values of the point \(P(x,y)\) will be important in the definitions of the trigonometric functions.

    Subsection 3.1.2 Definitions

    We will focus on six trigonometric functions: sine, cosine, tangent, cosecant, secant, and cotangent. It is important to be familiar with their definitions, both in terms of right angled triangles, and in terms of angles in standard position in a circle.

    Definitions of trigonometric functions - right angled triangles.

    Consider a right angled triangle, where one of the non-right angles is \(\theta \text{.}\) This means \(0 \lt \theta \lt \frac{\pi}{2}\text{.}\) Then the trigonometric functions are defined as follows:
    • Sine: \(\sin \theta = \) Opposite / Hypothenuse
    • Cosine: \(\cos \theta = \) Adjacent / Hypothenuse
    • Tangent: \(\tan \theta = \) Opposite / Adjacent
    Study tip:
    You should memorize all defitions of trigonometric functions, if you don’t know them already. It is important to know both the definitions in terms of right angled triangles AND the definitions in terms of the circle.

    Definitions of trigonometric functions - general definition.

    Let \(\theta \) be any angle in standard position in a circle centered at \((0,0)\text{.}\) With \(x, y\) and \(r\) as in Figure 3.3, we make the following definitions:
    • \(\displaystyle \sin \theta = \frac{y}{r}\)
    • \(\displaystyle \cos \theta = \frac{x}{r}\)
    • \(\displaystyle \tan \theta = \frac{y}{x}\)

    Trigonometric functions - relationships.

    The following relationships are all important.
    • \(\displaystyle \tan \theta = \frac{\sin \theta}{\cos \theta}\)
    • \(\displaystyle \csc \theta = \frac{1}{\sin \theta}\)
    • \(\displaystyle \sec \theta = \frac{1}{\cos \theta}\)
    • \(\displaystyle \cot \theta = \frac{1}{\tan \theta} = \frac{\cos \theta}{\sin \theta}\)
    Study tip:
    You should take the time to memorize all of the listed relationships between trigonometric functions, if you don’t know them already.

    Checkpoint 3.5. Definitions of \(\sec \theta\text{,}\) \(\csc \theta\) and \(\cot \theta\).

    Use the listed relationships between trigonomtric functions to find the definitions of secant, cosecant and cotangent. Write down lists of definitions first in terms of right angled triangles, then in terms of \(x, y, r\) in a circle.
    Hint.
    We know that \(\csc \theta = \frac{1}{\sin \theta}\text{.}\) Therefore, in terms of right angled triangles, \(\csc \theta = \frac{1}{opp/hyp} = \frac{hyp}{opp}\text{.}\) Now do the same for secant and cotangent.
    In terms of \(x, y, r\text{,}\) we see that \(\csc \theta = \frac{1}{\sin \theta} = \frac{1}{y/r} = \frac{r}{y}\text{.}\) Now do the same for secant and cotangent.

    Subsection 3.1.3 Evaluating using special triangles

    Being able to evaluate trigonometric functions for common angles will be important in many contexts and examples in calculus. There are several ways to do this. One of the easest is to start with two special triangles.
    Study tip:
    You should take the time to memorize these two special triangles if you haven’t already. They will be useful in finding many trigonometric values. You should practice drawing them on a blank piece of paper until you know them.

    Example 3.8. Evaluating using special triangles.

    Use the special triangles to find the value of \(\cos \left(\frac{\pi}{3}\right) \text{.}\)
    Answer.
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{\cos \left(\frac{\pi}{3}\right)= \frac{1}{2}} \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    We use the special triangle that has the angle \(\frac{\pi}{3} \text{.}\) We also know from the definition of \(\cos \theta \) that \(\cos \theta = \) adjacent / hypothenuse. Reading off the values in the apecial triangle we see that
    \begin{equation*} \cos \left(\frac{\pi}{3}\right) = \frac{adj}{hyp} = \frac{1}{2} \end{equation*}
    The final result is:
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{\cos \left(\frac{\pi}{3}\right)= \frac{1}{2}} \end{equation*}

    Subsection 3.1.4 Evaluating using the coordinate system

    If an angle is not in the first quadrant, then it is not enough to use the special triangles. But we can use the special triangles in combination with angles in standard position in a coordinate system.

    Example 3.12. Evaluating at \(\frac{2\pi}{3}\).

    Find exact values for \(\sin \left(\frac{2\pi}{3}\right), \ \cos \left(\frac{2\pi}{3}\right) \) and \(\tan \left(\frac{2\pi}{3}\right)\) without using a calculator.
    Answer.
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{\sin \left(\frac{2\pi}{3}\right) = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}, \cos \left(\frac{2\pi}{3}\right) = - \frac{1}{2}, \tan \left(\frac{2\pi}{3}\right) = - \sqrt{3}} \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    Start by drawing a coordinate system with a circle centered at the origin (not necessarily the unit circle). Place the angle \(\theta= \frac{2\pi}{3} \) in standard position (see Figure 3.2).
    The part of angle \(\frac{2\pi}{3} \) which is in quadrant II, is \(\frac{2\pi}{3}\text{.}\) Therefore, place the special triangle with \(\frac{\pi}{6}\) (see Figure 3.7) in quadrant II:
    Reading from the figure, we see that for the angle \(\theta= \frac{2\pi}{3} \) we can take:
    \begin{equation*} x = -1, y = \sqrt{3}, r = 2. \end{equation*}
    Then, using the definitions in Subsection 3.1.2, we have the following:
    \begin{equation*} \sin \left(\frac{2\pi}{3}\right) = \frac{y}{r} = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \cos \left(\frac{2\pi}{3}\right) = \frac{x}{r} = - \frac{1}{2} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \tan \left(\frac{2\pi}{3}\right) = \frac{x}{r} = - \sqrt{3} \end{equation*}

    We follow similar steps to the previous example. Start by drawing a coordinate system with a circle, but this time placing the angle \(\theta= -\frac{\pi}{6} \) in standard position (see Figure 3.2). Because it is a negative angle, it is in the clockwise direction.

    \begin{equation*} \sin \left(-\frac{\pi}{6}\right) = \frac{y}{r} = -\frac{1}{2} \end{equation*}

    Subsection 3.1.5 Evaluating at \(0, \pi/2, \pi\) etc.

    If you know the graphs of \(y=\sin \theta\) and \(y=\cos \theta\text{,}\) then it is easy to find the values of all trigonometric functions for any multiple of \(\frac{\pi}{2}\text{.}\) Trigonometic graphs are discussed more in Section 3.2, but you may already remember the graphs of \(y=\sin \theta\) and \(y=\cos \theta\text{:}\)
    Study tip:
    You should memorize these graphs, and the graph of \(y=\tan \theta\text{.}\) A lot of information can be seen quickly in a graph, and for most people it’s easier to memorize a graph than a long list of facts.

    Example 3.19. Evaluating \(\cos \left(\frac{3\pi}{2}\right)\).

    Evaluate \(\cos \left(\frac{3\pi}{2}\right) \) without using a calculator.
    Answer.
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{\cos \left(\frac{3\pi}{2}\right)= 0} \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    Option 1: Looking at the graph of \(y=\cos \theta\text{,}\) we see that at \(\frac{3\pi}{2}\text{,}\) the graph crosses the horizontal axis. Therefore the value is \(0\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{\cos \left(\frac{3\pi}{2}\right)= 0} \end{equation*}
    Option 2: Another option would be to look at angles on the unit circle (in standard position, see Figure 3.2).
    From the figure we see that for the angle \(\theta =\frac{3\pi}{2}\text{,}\) we have \(x=0\text{,}\) \(y=-1\) and \(r=1\) (\(r\) is always 1 on the unit circle). Also, \(y=\cos \theta = \frac{x}{r}\) (recall from Subsection 3.1.2). This means \(y=\cos \left(\frac{3\pi}{2}\right) = \frac{0}{1}= 0\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{\cos \left(\frac{3\pi}{2}\right)= 0} \end{equation*}

    Example 3.20. Evaluating \(\sec \left(4\pi\right)\).

    Evaluate \(\sec \left(4\pi\right)\) without using a calculator.
    Answer.
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{\sec (4\pi))= 1} \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    We know that \(\sec \theta = \frac{1}{\cos \theta}\) so therefore \(\sec \left(4\pi\right) = \frac{1}{\cos \left(4\pi\right)}\text{.}\) We can evaluate \(\cos (4\pi)\) by looking at the graph of \(\cos \theta\text{,}\) or by using the unit circle. Either way, \(\cos (4\pi)=1\text{.}\) Therefore \(\sec \left(4\pi\right) = \frac{1}{\cos \left(4\pi\right)} = \frac{1}{1} = 1\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{\sec (4\pi))= 1} \end{equation*}

    Subsection 3.1.6 Summary

    The types of angles we have evaluated trigonometric functions at, are summarized here.

    Common angles.

    You should be able to evaluate all 6 trigonometric functions at the following common angles:
    • First quadrant: \(\frac{\pi}{6}, \frac{\pi}{4}, \frac{\pi}{3} \) (Subsection 3.1.3)
    • Corresponding angles in other quadrants, such as \(\frac{5\pi}{6}, \frac{3\pi}{4}, \frac{5\pi}{3}, \) etc. (Subsection 3.1.4)
    • Multiples of \(\frac{\pi}{2}\text{,}\) such as \(0, \frac{\pi}{2}, \pi, \frac{3\pi}{2}, 2\pi, 3\pi \) etc. (Subsection 3.1.5)
    • Similar negative angles, such as \(-\frac{\pi}{6}, -\frac{\pi}{2}, -\frac{3\pi}{4} \) etc. (Subsection 3.1.4 and Subsection 3.1.5)
