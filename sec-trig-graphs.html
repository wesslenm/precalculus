    Section 4.2 Trigonometric graphs

    Objectives

    • Recognize and be able to sketch trigonometric graphs.
    • Use the graphs of trigonometric functions to read off important information.
    • Determine symmetries of trigonometric functions.
    • Determine amplitude, period and phase shift.

    Where will this be used in calculus?

    Trigonometric functions are used to model many phenomena of a periodic nature, and are therefore an important tool in calculus as well. Graphs can be used to get an overview of a function, or to recall specific information easily. Some examples are:
    • Finding the area between curves or the volume of certain solids is an important part of integral calculus. Being familiar with trigonometric graphs is essential.
    • Analyzing amplitude, period and phase shift of a periodic wave is imprtant for understanding the characterisics of the functions and of any situation that is modeled.
    In this section we discuss the graphs of trigonometric functions, and some properties of the graphs.

    One benefit of graphs is that a lot of information about a function can be seen quickly, by looking at a graph. Some examples are seen below.
    Study tip:
    Try answering the questions in Example 4.26 yourself before looking at the solution.

    Example 4.26. Properties from graphs.

    Use the graphs of trigonometric functions to find the following information:
    (a) \(\tan 0\)
    (b) All values of \(x\) in the interval \([-2\pi, 2\pi]\) where \(\sin x = 0\text{.}\)
    (c) All values of \(x\) in the interval \([-2\pi, 2\pi]\) where \(\tan x\) is undefined.
    Solution.
    Looking at the graphs given earlier in this section, we can easily find the information.
    (a) \(\tan 0 = 0\text{,}\) as seen from the graph of \(y=\tan \theta\text{.}\)
    (b) Looking at the graph of \(y=\sin \theta\text{,}\) we can see that it crosses the \(x\)-axis 5 times in the inteval \([-2\pi, 2\pi]\text{,}\) namely at the \(x\)-values \(x=-2\pi, x=-\pi, x=0, x=\pi\) and \(x=2\pi\text{.}\)
    (c) Looking at the graph of \(y=\tan \theta\text{,}\) we can see that it is undefined 4 times in the inteval \([-2\pi, 2\pi]\text{,}\) namely at the \(x\)-values \(x=-\frac{3\pi}{2}, x=-\frac{\pi}{2}, x=\frac{\pi}{2}\) and \(x=\frac{3\pi}{2}\text{.}\)

    Graphs also illustrate symmetries. For example, \(y=\sin \theta\) and \(y=\tan \theta\) are odd functions, while \(y=\cos \theta\) is an even function.
    Recall that a function \(f(x)\) is an odd function if \(f(-x)=-f(x)\) for all \(x\) in the domain of \(f\text{.}\) Visually, this means that the graph has 180 degree symmetry about the origin.
    Also, \(f(x)\) is an even function if \(f(-x)=f(x)\) for all \(x\) in the domain of \(f\text{.}\) This means that the graph of \(f\) has reflective symmetry about the \(y\)-axis.

    Example 4.28. Symmetries.

    Which of the following statements are true, and which are false?
    (a) \(y=\tan x\) is an odd function.
    (b) \(y=\sec x\) is an odd function.
    (c) \(\sin \left(\frac{3}{8}\right) = - \sin \left(-\frac{3}{8}\right)\)
    Answer.
    (a) True
    (b) False
    (c) True
    Solution.
    (a) This is true. Looking at the graph we see that it is symmetric about the origin.
    (b) This is false. Looking at the graph we see that it is symmetric about the \(y\)-axis, so this is an even function, not odd.
    (c) This is true. This is a property of odd functions and \(y=\sin x \) is an odd function.

    Subsection 4.2.3 Transformations of graphs

    Transformations of graphs in general are reviewed in Section 2.3. Here we discuss some transformations specifically in terms of trigonometric functions.

    Example 4.30. Basic transformations GRAPHS NEEDED.

    Use a graphing tool to graph \(y=2\cos x\) and \(y=\cos (2x)\text{.}\) How are they different from the graph of \(y=\cos x\text{?}\)
    Solution.
    INSERT GRAPHS
    Looking at the graph of \(y=2\cos x\text{,}\) we see that it looks very similar to the graph of \(y=\cos x\text{,}\) except that the \(y\)-values range between -2 and 2, instead of -1 and 1.
    Looking at the graph of \(y=\cos (2x))\text{,}\) we see that it again looks similar to the graph of \(y=\cos x\text{,}\) except this time the persiod is \(\pi\) instead of \(2\pi\text{.}\) The oscillations occur `twice as fast’ compared to the graph of \(y=\cos x)\text{.}\)
    Generalizing what we saw in Example 4.30, the graph of \(y=A \cos x\) (for \(A \not= 0\)) oscillates between \(A\) and \(-A\text{.}\) Similarly for the graph of \(y=A \sin x\text{.}\) The value \(A\) is called the amplitude of the sine or cosine curve.
    The graphs of \(y=\cos (kx)\) and \(y=\sin (kx)\) (for \(k \not= 0\)) have periods of \(2\pi /k\text{.}\) This is because compared to \(y=\cos x\) and \(y=\sin x\text{,}\) the graphs of \(y=\cos (kx)\) and \(y=\sin (kx)\) are compressed horizontally by a factor of \(k\text{.}\)
    Compared to \(y=\cos x\text{,}\) the graph of \(y=\cos (x+c)\) is shifted \(c\) units left if \(c > 0\) and right if \(c\) is less than 0. Here, \(c\) is called the phase shift.

    Example 4.31. Amplitude, period, phase shift.

    Find the amplitude, period and phase shift for the curve
    \begin{equation*} y = 3 \sin (4x-7) \end{equation*}
    Answer.
    The amplitude is \(3\text{.}\)
    The period is \(\frac{\pi}{2}\text{.}\)
    The phase shift is \(\frac{7}{4}\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    The amplitude is given by the constant multiplied by \(sin \theta\text{,}\) in this case 3.
    To find the period, notice that compared to \(y=\sin x\text{,}\) the graph of \(y = 3 \sin (4x-7)\) is compressed by a factor of \(4\text{.}\) This means the period is divided by \(4\text{.}\) The period of \(y=\sin x\) is \(2\pi\text{,}\) which means the persiod of \(y = 3 \sin (4x-7)\) is \(\frac{2\pi}{4}= \frac{\pi}{2}\text{.}\)
    To find the phase shift, we need to consider \((4x-7)\text{.}\) It is easier to see the phase shift if we first factor the \(4\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} y = 3 \sin (4x-7) = 3 \sin \left(4\left(x-\frac{7}{4}\right)\right) \end{equation*}
    Now we see that the curve has been shifted \(\frac{7}{4}\) units to the right, so the phase shift is \(\frac{7}{4}\text{.}\)
