    Section 3.2 Trigonometric graphs

    Objectives

    • Recognize and be able to sketch trigonometric graphs.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Use the graphs of trigonometric functions to interpret important information.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Determine symmetries of trigonometric functions.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Determine amplitude, period, midline and phase shift.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Where will this be used in calculus?

    Trigonometric functions are used to model a variety of periodic phenomena, and are therefore an important tool in calculus. Graphs can be used to get an overview of a function, or to obtain specific information easily. Some examples are:
    🔗
    • Finding the area between curves or the volume of certain solids in integral calculus.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Use amplitude, period, midline and phase shift of a periodic function to describe and analyze the characterisics of a modeled situation.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    In this section, we discuss the graphs of trigonometric functions, and some properties of their graphs.
    🔗

    Subsection 3.2.1 Sinusoidal Graphs and Notation

    A sinusoidal function is similar to \(y=\sin x\) but possibly shifted, stretched, reflected etc. Below is an example of a graph of a sinusoidal function.
    🔗

    Notation.

    The image shows an example of a sinusoidal graph. To describe the graph, we use some specific notation.
    🔗
    • The midline is the horizontal line at middle value between the maximum and the minumum values of the graph.
      🔗
      🔗
    • The amplitude is the distance between the midline and the maximum value. This is also equal to the distance between the midline and the minimum value
      🔗
      🔗
    • Sinusoidal functions are periodic, which means that they repeat after a certain interval. The length of that interval is called the period. Therefore we can say that the period of a sinusoidal function is the distance between two consequitive maximum points (which is also the same as the distance between two consequitive minimum points).
      🔗
      🔗
    • We also use phase shift to describe sinusoidal graphs. The phase shift is the horizontal distance that the graph has been shifted, compared to its related non-shifted function \(y=\sin (kx)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      A phase shift can also be called a horizontal translation or sometimes a horizontal shift. The term ``horizontal translation’’ applies to any graphs translated horizontally, while the term ``phase shift’’ is used only for sinusoidal graphs.
      🔗
      🔗

    Graphs also illustrate symmetries. For example, \(y=\sin \theta\) and \(y=\tan \theta\) are odd functions, while \(y=\cos \theta\) is an even function.
    🔗
    Recall that a function \(f(x)\) is an odd function if \(f(-x)=-f(x)\) for all \(x\) in the domain of \(f\text{.}\) Visually, this means that the graph has 180 degree symmetry about the origin. (If you rotate the graph 180 degree around the origin, the graph still looks the same.)
    🔗
    Also, \(f(x)\) is an even function if \(f(-x)=f(x)\) for all \(x\) in the domain of \(f\text{.}\) This means that the graph of \(f\) has reflective symmetry about the \(y\)-axis. (If you rotate the graph 180 degree around the origin, the graph still looks the same.)
    🔗
    Note that the reflective symmetry must be specifically about the \(y\)-axis, for the function to be called an even function. Similarly an odd function must have rotational symmetry about the origin, not some other point.
    🔗

    Subsection 3.2.4 Transformations of graphs

    Transformations of graphs in general may be reviewed in another section, or you may recall shifts, reflections and stretches/compressions from your prior studies. Here we discuss some transformations specifically in terms of trigonometric functions.
    🔗

    Example 3.21. Basic transformations.

    Use a graphing tool (for example DESMOS) to graph \(y=2\cos x\) and \(y=\cos (2x)\text{.}\) How are each of them different from the graph of \(y=\cos x\text{?}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Let’s start by looking at the graphs of \(y=2\cos x\) and \(y=\cos x\) together.
    🔗
    Looking at the graph of \(y=2\cos x\text{,}\) we see that it looks very similar to the graph of \(y=\cos x\text{,}\) except that the \(y\)-values range between -2 and 2, instead of -1 and 1. The graph of \(y=2\cos x\) is streched vertically by a factor of 2, compared to the graph of \(y=\cos x\text{.}\) This is because all outputs are multiplied by 2 (compared to the corrsponding outputs of \(y=\cos x\)).
    🔗
    Now let’s compare the graphs of \(y=\cos (2x)\) and \(y=\cos x\text{:}\)
    🔗
    Looking at the graph of \(y=\cos (2x)\text{,}\) we see that it again looks similar to the graph of \(y=\cos x\text{,}\) except its period is \(\pi\) instead of \(2\pi\text{.}\) The oscillations occur `twice as fast’ compared to the graph of \(y=\cos x\text{.}\) The graph of \(y=\cos (2x)\) is compressed horizontally by a factor of 2, compared to the graph of \(y=\cos x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Generalizing what we saw in Example 3.21, the graph of \(y=A \cos x\) (for \(A \not= 0)\) oscillates between \(A\) and \(-A\) (because it has been stretched vertically). Similarly for the graph of \(y=A \sin x\text{.}\) The number \(A\) is the amplitude of the sine or cosine curve.
    🔗

    Generalizing Example 3.21 and Checkpoint 3.22 we can summarize some results as follows:
    🔗

    Transformations.

    Consider the functions
    \begin{equation*} f\left(x\right)=a\sin\left(kx-d\right)+c \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} f\left(x\right)=a\cos\left(kx-d\right)+c \end{equation*}
    🔗
    • If \(a\not= 0\text{,}\) the graphs are streched or compressed (compared to the graphs of \(f\left(x\right)=\sin x\) and \(f\left(x\right)=\cos x\)) so that their amplitude is given by \(|a|\) (the absolute value of \(|a|\)). If \(a\) is negative then the curve has been reflected in the \(x\)-axis as well as stretched or compressed.
      🔗
      🔗
    • The graphs are shifted up \(c\) units if \(c\) is positive, and down \(c\) units if \(c\) is negative.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Compared to \(y=\sin x\text{,}\) the graph of \(y=a\sin\left(kx-d\right)+c\) is stretched if \(k\) is less than 1, and compressed if \(k\) is more than 1. In either case if \(k\not= 0\text{,}\) the period is \(\frac{2\pi}{k}\text{.}\) Similarly for cosine.
      🔗
      🔗
    • The period of \(y=\tan x\) is \(\pi\) so the period of \(y=a\tan\left(kx-d\right)+c\) is \(\frac{\pi}{k}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • To see the effect of \(d\text{,}\) it is easiest to first factor \(k\text{:}\)
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} y=a\sin\left(kx-d\right)+c = a\sin\left(k\left(x-\frac{d}{k}\right)\right)+c. \end{equation*}
      🔗
      Compared to \(y=\sin x\text{,}\) the graph of \(y=a\sin\left(k\left(x-\frac{d}{k}\right)\right)+c\) is shifted \(\frac{d}{k}\) units left if \(\frac{d}{k} > 0\) and right if \(\frac{d}{k}\) is less than 0. Here, \(\frac{d}{k}\) is called the phase shift (see Subsection 3.2.1).
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.23. Amplitude, period, phase shift.

    Find the amplitude, period and phase shift for the curve
    \begin{equation*} y = 3 \sin (4x-7) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Answer.
    The amplitude is \(3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The period is \(\frac{\pi}{2}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The phase shift is \(\frac{7}{4}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    The amplitude is given by the constant multiplied by \(\sin \theta\text{,}\) in this case 3.
    🔗
    To find the period, notice that compared to \(y=\sin x\text{,}\) the graph of \(y = 3 \sin (4x-7)\) is compressed by a factor of \(4\text{.}\) This means the period is divided by \(4\text{.}\) The period of \(y=\sin x\) is \(2\pi\text{,}\) which means the persiod of \(y = 3 \sin (4x-7)\) is \(\frac{2\pi}{4}= \frac{\pi}{2}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    To find the phase shift, we need to consider \((4x-7)\text{.}\) It is easier to see the phase shift if we first factor the \(4\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} y = 3 \sin (4x-7) = 3 \sin \left(4\left(x-\frac{7}{4}\right)\right) \end{equation*}
    Now we see that the curve has been shifted \(\frac{7}{4}\) units to the right, so the phase shift is \(\frac{7}{4}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
