    Section 1.2 Rational Expressions

    Objectives

    • Simplify and re-work rational expressions.
    • Simplify and work with fractions.
    • Add, subtract, multiply and divide rational expressions.
    • Simplify and rewrite complex fractions and complex rational expressions.

    Where will this be used in calculus?

    Here are several specific calculus examples where working with rational expressions will be useful:
    • After using rules to differentiate a function (especially the quotient rule) the result is often a rational expression or complex rational expression which needs to be simplified.
    • When using implicit differentiation the process often involves working with and simplifying complex rational expressions.
    • Integration using the technique called Partial Fractions, involves rational expressions.
    • In Sequences and Series, using the Limit Comparison Test and the Ratio Test often involves working with multiple complex rational expressions.
    The quotient of two polynomials is called a rational expression. For example, lets look at the following rational expression:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x^3+3x^2}{x^2+2x-3} \end{equation*}
    Here, \(x^3+3x^2\) is the numerator, and \(x^2+2x-3\) is the denominator. In this section we will see how to add, subtract, multiply, divide and simplify rational expressions. The rules we will follow are similar to rules regarding working with fractions (such as for example simplifying \(\frac{3}{4} - \frac{1}{5}\)).

    Subsection 1.2.1 Common factors

    Cancelling common factors is one way to simplify a rational expression. Not all expressions have common factors, but it is often useful to check by factoring both the numerator and the denominator.
    A factor is part of an expression which is multiplied by the rest of the expression.

    Example 1.1. Common factor.

    Simplify the rational epression
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x^3+3x^2}{x^2+2x-3}. \end{equation*}
    Answer.
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{\frac{x^3+3x^2}{x^2+2x-3}=\frac{x^2}{x-1}} \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    Let’s factor both the numerator and the denominator to see if there are any common factors.
    \begin{equation*} x^3+3x^2 = x^2(x+3) \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} x^2+2x-3= (x-1)(x+3). \end{equation*}
    Next, lets look at the expression in the question and cancel any common factors:
    \begin{align*} \frac{x^3+3x^2}{x^2+2x-3} &= \frac{x^2(x+3)}{(x-1)(x+3)}\\ &= \frac{x^2}{x-1}\text{.} \end{align*}
    So, in the end, we see that
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{\frac{x^3+3x^2}{x^2+2x-3}=\frac{x^2}{x-1}} \end{equation*}

    The cancelation in Example 1.1 is based on the following rule, which says that if both the numerator and the denominator has the same FACTOR, it can be cancelled. Note that what you are canceling must be multiplied (or divided) by the rest of the numerator and denominator, NOT added or subtracted.

    Rule.

    For any real numbers \(a\text{,}\) \(b\text{,}\) \(c\) and \(k\) where \(b\not= 0\) and \(c\not= 0\text{,}\) it is true that
    \begin{equation} \frac{ac}{bc}=\frac{a}{b}\tag{1.2.1} \end{equation}
    and
    \begin{equation} \frac{ak}{b}=\frac{a}{b} \cdot k\tag{1.2.2} \end{equation}
    This is true both if \(a\text{,}\) \(b\text{,}\) \(c\) and \(k\) are be constants (numbers) and/or expressions involving variables.

    Common Error.

    In general, the following are not true.
    \begin{equation*} \frac{a+c}{b+c}\not=\frac{a}{b} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \frac{a-c}{b-c}\not=\frac{a}{b} \end{equation*}
    Note that (1.2.1) can be useful in ``both directions’’. Sometimes it is useful to multiply both numerator and denominator in a fraction. You can do that, as long as you multiply both numerator and denominator by the same expression. We will see examples of that later.

    Subsection 1.2.2 Multiplying and dividing rational expressions

    To multiplying rational expressions we use the same ideas and rules as when multiplying numerical fractions. The numerators are multipled, and the denominators are multiplied. For example
    \begin{equation*} \frac{2}{3}\times \frac{5}{7} = \frac{10}{21} \end{equation*}
    Sometimes it might be worth simplifying the result, or perhaps simplifying before multiplying (if it’s easy enough to do so).

    Technique.

    To multiply two rational expressions, multiply the numerators to find the numerator of the product. Then multiply the denominators to find the denominator of the product.
    \begin{equation} \frac{a}{b} \times \frac{c}{d}= \frac{ac}{bd}\tag{1.2.3} \end{equation}
    where \(a\text{,}\) \(b\text{,}\) \(c\) and \(d\) could be constants (numbers) and/or expressions involving variables.

    Example 1.5. Multiplying rational expressions.

    Multiply the rational expressions and simplify the answer:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{3x^2+2}{x^2+2x-3} \cdot \frac{x+10}{x^2-x+2} \end{equation*}
    Answer.
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{\frac{3x^2+2}{x^2+2x-3} \cdot \frac{x+10}{x^2-x+2}=\frac{3x^3+ 30x^2 +2x+20}{x^4+x^3-3x^2+7x-6}} \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    Multiply numerators separately, then denominators separately, as described in (1.2.3).
    \begin{equation*} \frac{3x^2+2}{x^2+2x-3} \cdot \frac{x+10}{x^2-x+2} = \frac{(3x^2+2)(x+10)}{(x^2+2x-3)(x^2-x+2)} \end{equation*}
    Note that it is important to use brackets, to show that all of \(3x^2+2 \) is multiplied by all of \(x+10 \) (and similarly for the denominator). Next, expend the brackets and simplify:
    \begin{align*} \frac{3x^2+2}{x^2+2x-3} \cdot \frac{x+10}{x^2-x+2} &= \frac{(3x^2+2)(x+10)}{(x^2+2x-3)(x^2-x+2)}\\ &= \frac{3x^3+ 30x^2 +2x+20}{x^4+x^3-3x^2+7x-6} \end{align*}
    There is no further simplification that can be made. So the answer is
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{\frac{3x^2+2}{x^2+2x-3} \cdot \frac{x+10}{x^2-x+2}=\frac{3x^3+ 30x^2 +2x+20}{x^4+x^3-3x^2+7x-6}} \end{equation*}

    \begin{equation*} \frac{2}{3} / \frac{5}{7} = \frac{2}{3} \times \frac{7}{5} = \frac{14}{25} \end{equation*}

    Subsection 1.2.3 Adding and Subtracting rational expressions

    Let’s start by looking at a numerical example of adding two fractions, for example \(\frac{5}{6} + \frac{3}{8}\text{.}\) To add fractions, we need to use a common denominator, then add the resulting numerators. The least common multiple of 6 and 8 is 24, so let’s use that as the common denominator in this case:
    \begin{align*} \frac{5}{6} + \frac{3}{8} &= \frac{20}{24}+\frac{9}{24}\\ &= \frac{20+9}{24}\\ &= \frac{29}{24} \end{align*}
    The general steps for adding rational expressions are the same. Also, the technique is the same for adding and for subtracting.

    Technique.

    To add or subtract two rational expressions:
    1. Find a common denominator. (It may be useful to factor the denominators.)
    2. Multiply each rational expression to change the denominators to the common denominator.
    3. Add or subtract the numerators.
    4. Simplify if needed.

    Example 1.11. Subtracting rational expressions.

    Subtract the rational expressions and simplify the answer:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x}{x^2-1} - \frac{2}{x^2+x-2} \end{equation*}
    Answer.
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{\frac{x}{x^2-1} - \frac{2}{x^2+x-2}=\frac{x^2-2}{(x-1)(x+1)(x+2)}} \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    We need find a common denominator. We could use any common denominator. For example we could simply use
    \begin{equation*} (x^2-1)\cdot(x^2+x-2) \end{equation*}
    but this means we need to expand these brackets and it would lead to high answers in the answer, which may be difficult to simplify. It is often more efficient to use the Least Common Decominator (LCD). To find the LCD, we first facor both denominators:
    \begin{equation*} x^2-1 = (x-1)(x+1). \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x^2+x-2 = (x-1)(x+2). \end{equation*}
    This means the LCD is
    \begin{equation*} (x-1)(x+1)(x+2). \end{equation*}
    Next, multiply the first rational expression by \(\frac{x+2}{x+2} \) and the second rational expression by \(\frac{x+1}{x+1} \) so that both are on the same common denominator. Note that multiplying by \(\frac{x+2}{x+2} \) is the same as multiplying by 1. It does not change the value of the expression, it just re-writes it in a different format.
    \begin{align*} \frac{x}{x^2-1} - \frac{2}{x^2+x-2} &= \frac{x}{(x-1)(x+1)} \cdot \frac{x+2}{x+2} - \frac{2}{(x+2)(x-1)} \cdot \frac{x+1}{x+1}\\ &= \frac{x(x+2)}{(x^2-1)(x+2)} - \frac{2(x+1)}{(x^2+x-2)(x+1)}\\ &= \frac{x(x+2)- 2(x+1)}{(x-1)(x+1)(x+2)}\\ &= \frac{x^2+2x-2x-2}{(x-1)(x+1)(x+2)}\\ &= \frac{x^2-2}{(x-1)(x+1)(x+2)}\text{.} \end{align*}
    So the answer is
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{\frac{x}{x^2-1} - \frac{2}{x^2+x-2}=\frac{x^2-2}{(x-1)(x+1)(x+2)}} \end{equation*}
    It is ok to leave the answer in this format, or to expand the denominator (or factor the numerator if you prefer).

    Subsection 1.2.4 Complex rational expressions

    A rational expression that contains a rational expression within the numerator or denominator (or both), is called a complex rational expression. To simplify a complex rational expression, start by simplifying the numberator, then the denominator, and after that is done you can simplify the overall expression.

    Example 1.15. Simplifying a complex rational expression.

    Simplify the complex rational expression:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\frac{x}{y}+1}{1+\frac{2}{x}}. \end{equation*}
    Answer.
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{\frac{\frac{x}{y}+1}{1+\frac{2}{x}}=\frac{x^2 + xy}{xy+2y}} \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    Simplify the numerator to make it a single fraction:
    \begin{align*} \frac{x}{y}+1 &= \frac{x}{y}+\frac{y}{y}\\ &= \frac{x+y}{y}\text{.} \end{align*}
    Simplify the denominator to make it a single fraction:
    \begin{align*} 1+\frac{2}{x} &= \frac{x}{x}+\frac{2}{x}\\ &= \frac{x+2}{x}\text{.} \end{align*}
    Now we are ready to simplify the complex rational expression. Remember that to divide by a rationl expression, multiply by the reciprocal (see (1.2.4)).
    \begin{align*} \frac{\frac{x}{y}+1}{1+\frac{2}{x}} &= \frac{\frac{x+y}{y}}{\frac{x+2}{x}}\\ &= \frac{x+y}{y} \cdot \frac{x}{x+2} \\ &= \frac{(x+y)x}{y(x+2)} \\ &= \frac{x^2 + xy}{xy+2y} \end{align*}
    There is no further simplification that can be made. So the final answer is
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{\frac{\frac{x}{y}+1}{1+\frac{2}{x}}=\frac{x^2 + xy}{xy+2y}} \end{equation*}
    Let’s summarize the steps we took in Example 1.15:

    Technique.

    To simplify complex rational expressions:
    1. Simplify the numerator to make it a single rational expression.
    2. Simplify the denominator to make it a single rational expression.
    3. Divide the two expressions, by multiplying of the reciprocal of the denominator (as in (1.2.4)).
    4. Simplify if needed.

    Subsection 1.2.5 Additional Examples

    Here are a few examples that use several of the techniques and rules seen above.
    You are strongly encouraged to try the examples first on your own, before looking at the worked solutions.

    Example 1.19. A complex rational expression with division.

    Simplify the expression:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\left(\frac{\frac{1}{x}+1}{x^2+3x}\right)}{4x}. \end{equation*}
    Answer.
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{\frac{\left(\frac{\frac{1}{x}+1}{x^2+3x}\right)}{4x}=\frac{1+x}{4x^4+12x^3}} \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    Consider the overall expression. We can use (1.2.2) to rewrite this into a simpler expression:
    \begin{align*} \frac{\left(\frac{\frac{1}{x}+1}{x^2+3x}\right)}{4x} &= \left(\frac{\frac{1}{x}+1}{x^2+3x}\right)\cdot\frac{1}{4x}\\ &= \frac{\left(\frac{1}{x}+1\right) \cdot 1 }{(x^2+3x)\cdot 4x}\\ &= \frac{\left(\frac{1}{x}+1\right)}{4x^3+12x^2} \end{align*}
    Now simplify the numerator to make it a single fraction:
    \begin{align*} \frac{1}{x}+1 &= \frac{1}{x}+\frac{x}{x}\\ &= \frac{1+x}{x} \end{align*}
    Now we have a rational expression where the numerator is a rational expression:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\left(\frac{1}{x}+1\right)}{4x^3+12x^2} = \frac{\left(\frac{1+x}{x}\right)}{4x^3+12x^2} \end{equation*}
    Again, we use (1.2.2) to rewrite this into a simpler expression:
    \begin{align*} \frac{\left(\frac{1+x}{x}\right)}{4x^3+12x^2} &= \left(\frac{1+x}{x}\right)\cdot \frac{1}{4x^3+12x^2}\\ &= \frac{(1+x) \cdot 1 }{x\cdot (4x^3+12x^2)}\\ &= \frac{1+x}{4x^4+12x^3}\text{.} \end{align*}
    The final answer is
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{\frac{\left(\frac{\frac{1}{x}+1}{x^2+3x}\right)}{4x}=\frac{1+x}{4x^4+12x^3}} \end{equation*}

    Example 1.20. A complex rational expression with two variables.

    Simplify the expression as much as possible. Assume that any factors you cancel are not zero.
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\frac{x^2-y^2}{xy}}{\frac{x}{y}-1}. \end{equation*}
    Answer.
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{\frac{\frac{x^2-y^2}{xy}}{\frac{x}{y}-1}=\frac{x+y}{x}} \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    Both the numerator and denominator are rational expressions. The numerator is already a single rational expression, but the denminator is not, so let’s rewrite the denominator on a common denominator: We can use (1.2.2) to rewrite this into a simpler expression:
    \begin{align*} \frac{x}{y}-1 &= \frac{x}{y}-\frac{y}{y}\\ &= \frac{x-y}{y} \end{align*}
    Now we have a rational expression divided by a rational expression, so we multiply by the reciprocal:
    \begin{align*} \frac{\frac{x^2-y^2}{xy}}{\frac{x}{y}-1} &= \frac{\frac{x^2-y^2}{xy}}{\frac{x-y}{y}}\\ &= \frac{x^2-y^2}{xy} \cdot \frac{y}{x-y}\\ &= \frac{(x^2-y^2)y}{xy(x-y)}\text{.} \end{align*}
    We can cancel a common factor of \(y\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} \frac{(x^2-y^2)y}{xy(x-y)} &= \frac{x^2-y^2}{x(x-y)}\text{.} \end{align*}
    We could make a note that \(y\not= 0\text{.}\) (This is because on the left side, \(y\) can not be 0 since it would result in division by 0). However, the statement of the question said that we can assume that any factors we cancel are not zero. The question also asked to simplify as much as possible. Let’s check for more common factors:
    \begin{align*} \frac{x^2-y^2}{x(x-y)} &= \frac{(x-y)(x+y)}{x(x-y)}\\ &= \frac{x+y}{x}\text{.} \end{align*}
    Again, we can assume that any factors we cancel are not zero, so we do not need to write \(x+y\not= 0\text{.}\) The final answer is
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{\frac{\frac{x^2-y^2}{xy}}{\frac{x}{y}-1}=\frac{x+y}{x}} \end{equation*}
