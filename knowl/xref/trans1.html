Example 3.36. Basic transformations.

Use a graphing tool (for example DESMOS) to graph first \(y=2\cos x\) and then \(y=\cos (2x)\text{.}\) How are each of them different from the graph of \(y=\cos x\text{?}\)
Solution.
Let’s start by looking at the graphs of \(y=2\cos x\) and \(y=\cos x\) together.
Looking at the graph of \(y=2\cos x\text{,}\) we see that it looks very similar to the graph of \(y=\cos x\text{,}\) except that the \(y\)-values range between -2 and 2, instead of -1 and 1. The graph of \(y=2\cos x\) is streched vertically by a factor of 2, compared to the graph of \(y=\cos x\text{.}\) This is because all outputs are multiplied by 2 (compared to the corrsponding outputs of \(y=\cos x\)).
Now let’s compare the graphs of \(y=\cos (2x)\) and \(y=\cos x\text{:}\)
Looking at the graph of \(y=\cos (2x))\text{,}\) we see that it again looks similar to the graph of \(y=\cos x\text{,}\) except its period is \(\pi\) instead of \(2\pi\text{.}\) The oscillations occur `twice as fast’ compared to the graph of \(y=\cos x\text{.}\) The graph of \(y=\cos (2x)\) is compressed horizontally by a factor of 2, compared to the graph of \(y=\cos x\text{.}\)
