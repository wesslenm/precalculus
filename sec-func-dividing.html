    Section 1.4 Dividing polynomials

    Objectives

    • Use long division to divide a polynomial by another polynomial.
    • Interpret the results of long division of polynomials.
    • Validate results (check answer).

    Where will this be used in calculus?

    Some specific examples of where long division of polynomials is used are:
    • Long division of polynomials can be useful when looking for certain types of asymptotes, which is important in graph sketching.
    • Long division of polynomials can be useful when factoring polynomials, for example when using the integration technique `partial fractions’.
    You may remember long division of large numbers, for example 5781 divided by 25. In this section we review how the technique of long division can be used to divide a polynomial by another polynomial.

    Subsection 1.4.1 Notation

    Suppose we want to divide the polynomial \(2x^3-7x^2+2x+7\) by \(2x+1\text{.}\) (We will actually do this later in Example 1.24.) The notation we will use is as follows:
    \(2x\) \(+\) \(1\) \(2x^3\) \(-\) \(7x^2\) \(+\) \(2x\) \(+\) \(7\)
    If dividing the polynomial \(2x^3-7x^2+2x+7\) by \(2x+1\text{,}\) then \(2x^3-7x^2+2x+7\) is called the dividend and \(2x+1\) is called the divisor. The answer to the division is called the quotient. When done, the quotient will appear at the top of the long division:
    quotient
    divisor dividend
    Sometimes there is a remainder if the dividend is not evenly divided by the divisor. For an example see Example 1.24.

    Subsection 1.4.2 Long division of polynomials

    Let’s start with a small example. Let’s divide \(x^2+5x+6\) by \(x+3\text{.}\) (You may be able to easily find out the answer by factoring \(x^2+5x+6\text{,}\) but instead let’s practice long division on this simple example.)

    Example 1.21. Polynomial long division.

    Use long division to divide the polynomial \(x^2+5x+6\) by \(x+3\text{.}\)
    Answer.
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x^2+5x+6}{x+3} = x+2 \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    Step 1.
    Setup:
    \(x\) \(+\) \(3\) \(x^2\) \(+\) \(5x\) \(+\) \(6\)
    Step 2.
    Question: \(x\) times what gives \(x^2 \text{?}\)
    Answer: \(x\text{.}\) So write \(x\) at the top. (\(x\) will be part of the final answer.)
    \(x\)
    \(x\) \(+\) \(3\) \(x^2\) \(+\) \(5x\) \(+\) \(6\)
    Step 3.
    Multiply \(x+3\) (the divisor) by \(x \text{.}\)
    \(x(x+3) = x^2 + 3x\text{.}\) Write this under the dividend.
    \(x\)
    \(x\) \(+\) \(3\) \(x^2\) \(+\) \(5x\) \(+\) \(6\)
    \(x^2\) \(+\) \(3x\)
    Step 4.
    Subtract \(x^2+3x\) from \(x^2 + 5x +6\text{.}\)
    Bring down the \(+6\text{.}\)
    \(x\)
    \(x\) \(+\) \(3\) \(x^2\) \(+\) \(5x\) \(+\) \(6\)
    \(-(x^2\) \(+\) \(3x)\)
    \(0\) \(+\) \(2x\) \(+\) \(6\)
    Step 5.
    Now repeat the process.
    Question: \(x\) times what equals \(2x \text{?}\)
    Answer: \(2\text{.}\) Therefore write \(2\) at the top.
    \(x\) \(+\) \(2\)
    \(x\) \(+\) \(3\) \(x^2\) \(+\) \(5x\) \(+\) \(6\)
    \(-(x^2\) \(+\) \(3x)\)
    \(0\) \(+\) \(2x\) \(+\) \(6\)
    Step 6.
    Multiply \(x+3\) (the divisor) by \(2 \text{.}\)
    \(2(x+3) = 2x + 6\text{.}\) Write this under the dividend.
    \(x\) \(+\) \(2\)
    \(x\) \(+\) \(3\) \(x^2\) \(+\) \(5x\) \(+\) \(6\)
    \(-(x^2\) \(+\) \(3x)\)
    \(0\) \(+\) \(2x\) \(+\) \(6\)
    \(2x\) \(+\) \(6\)
    Step 7.
    Subtract \(2x+6\) from \(2x+6\text{.}\)
    \(x\) \(+\) \(2\)
    \(x\) \(+\) \(3\) \(x^2\) \(+\) \(5x\) \(+\) \(6\)
    \(-(x^2\) \(+\) \(3x)\)
    \(0\) \(+\) \(2x\) \(+\) \(6\)
    \(-(2x\) \(+\) \(6)\)
    \(0\)
    Step 8.
    Now we read off the answer. The answer is \(x+2\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x^2+5x+6}{x+3}=x+2 \end{equation*}
    If the last step in the long division is not zero, we say that there is a remainder.
    When a polynomial \(f(x)\) is divided by a polynomial \(g(x)\text{,}\) with quotient \(q(x)\) and remainder \(r(x)\text{,}\) then the result can be written in the form:
    \begin{equation} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = q(x) + \frac{r(x)}{d(x)}\tag{1.4.1} \end{equation}

    Example 1.22. Interpreting the result.

    Suppose Nika has used long division to divide \(6x^2 -23x + 9\) by \(3x-1\text{.}\) Nika found that the quotient is \(2x-7\) and the remainder is \(2\text{.}\) Write the result in the format of (1.4.1).
    Answer.
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{\frac{6x^2 -23x + 9}{3x-1} = 2x-7 + \frac{2}{3x-1}} \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    The format in (1.4.1) is
    \begin{equation*} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = q(x) + \frac{r(x)}{d(x)} \end{equation*}
    In this example we have:
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = 6x^2 -23x + 9 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} g(x) = 3x-1 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} q(x) = 2x-7 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} r(x) = 2 \end{equation*}
    Therefore the result can be stated as:
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{\frac{6x^2 -23x + 9}{3x-1} = 2x-7 + \frac{2}{3x-1}} \end{equation*}

    Example 1.24. Polynomial long division with remainder.

    Use long division to divide the polynomial \(2x^3-7x^2+2x+7\) by \(2x+1\text{.}\) Find the quotient and the remainder (if there is one).
    Answer.
    \begin{equation*} \frac{2x^3-7x^2+2x+7}{2x+1} = x^2-4x+3 + \frac{4}{2x+1} \end{equation*}
    Quotient: \(x^2-4x+3\text{.}\)
    Remainder: 4
    Solution 1.
    Here is the worked out long division. In "Solution 2" you will find a breakdown of all the steps within the same long division.
    \(x^2\) \(-\) \(4x\) \(+\) \(3\)
    \(2x\) \(+\) \(1\) \(2x^3\) \(-\) \(7x^2\) \(+\) \(2x\) \(+\) \(7\)
    \(-(2x^3\) \(+\) \(x^2)\)
    \(0\) \(-\) \(8x^2\) \(+\) \(2x\)
    \(-(-8x^2\) \(-\) \(4x)\)
    \(6x\) \(+\) \(7\)
    \(-(6x\) \(+\) \(3)\)
    \(4\)
    This means that:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{2x^3-7x^2+2x+7}{2x+1} = x^2-4x+3 + \frac{4}{2x+1} \end{equation*}
    Quotient: \(x^2-4x+3\text{.}\)
    Remainder: 4
    Solution 2.
    This is a detailed solution with the steps outlined. Let’s follow a similar procedure to above.
    Step 1. Setup:
    \(2x\) \(+\) \(1\) \(2x^3\) \(-\) \(7x^2\) \(+\) \(2x\) \(+\) \(7\)
    Step 2.
    Question: \(2x\) times what gives \(2x^3 \text{?}\)
    Answer: \(x^2\text{.}\) So write \(x^2\) at the top (as part of the quotient).
    \(x^2\)
    \(2x\) \(+\) \(1\) \(2x^3\) \(-\) \(7x^2\) \(+\) \(2x\) \(+\) \(7\)
    Step 3.
    Multiply \(x^2\) by \(2x+1\text{.}\)
    \(x^2(2x+1) = 2x^3 + x^2\text{.}\) Write this under the dividend.
    \(x^2\)
    \(2x\) \(+\) \(1\) \(2x^3\) \(-\) \(7x^2\) \(+\) \(2x\) \(+\) \(7\)
    \(2x^3\) \(+\) \(x^2\)
    Step 4.
    Subtract \(2x^3+x^2\text{.}\)
    And bring down the next term: \(2x\text{.}\)
    \(x^2\)
    \(2x\) \(+\) \(1\) \(2x^3\) \(-\) \(7x^2\) \(+\) \(2x\) \(+\) \(7\)
    \(-(2x^3\) \(+\) \(x^2)\)
    \(0\) \(-\) \(8x^2\) \(+\) \(2x\)
    Step 5.
    Now repeat the process.
    Question: \(2x\) times what equals \(-8x^2 \text{?}\)
    Answer: \(-4x\text{.}\) Therefore write \(-4x\) at the top.
    \(x^2\) \(-\) \(4x\)
    \(2x\) \(+\) \(1\) \(2x^3\) \(-\) \(7x^2\) \(+\) \(2x\) \(+\) \(7\)
    \(-(2x^3\) \(+\) \(x^2)\)
    \(0\) \(-\) \(8x^2\) \(+\) \(2x\)
    Step 6.
    Multiply \(-4x\) by \(2x+1 \text{.}\)
    \(-4x(2x+1) = -8x^2-4x\text{.}\) Write this under the last line. Make sure that like terms line up (place \(x^2\)-terms below \(x^2\)-terms etc.)
    \(x^2\) \(-\) \(4x\)
    \(2x\) \(+\) \(1\) \(2x^3\) \(-\) \(7x^2\) \(+\) \(2x\) \(+\) \(7\)
    \(-(2x^3\) \(+\) \(x^2)\)
    \(0\) \(-\) \(8x^2\) \(+\) \(2x\)
    \(-8x^2\) \(-\) \(4x\)
    Step 7.
    Subtract \(-8x^2-4x\text{.}\) Remember that \(-(-8x^2-4x)= 8x^2+4x\text{.}\)
    Then bring down the 7.
    \(x^2\) \(-\) \(4x\)
    \(2x\) \(+\) \(1\) \(2x^3\) \(-\) \(7x^2\) \(+\) \(2x\) \(+\) \(7\)
    \(-(2x^3\) \(+\) \(x^2)\)
    \(0\) \(-\) \(8x^2\) \(+\) \(2x\)
    \(-(-8x^2\) \(-\) \(4x)\)
    \(6x\) \(+\) \(7\)
    Step 8.
    Repeat the process again.
    Question: \(2x\) times what equals \(6x \text{?}\)
    Answer: \(3\text{.}\) Therefore write \(3\) at the top.
    \(x^2\) \(-\) \(4x\) \(+\) \(3\)
    \(2x\) \(+\) \(1\) \(2x^3\) \(-\) \(7x^2\) \(+\) \(2x\) \(+\) \(7\)
    \(-(2x^3\) \(+\) \(x^2)\)
    \(0\) \(-\) \(8x^2\) \(+\) \(2x\)
    \(-(-8x^2\) \(-\) \(4x)\)
    \(6x\) \(+\) \(7\)
    Step 9.
    Multiply \(3\) by \(2x+1 \text{.}\)
    \(3(2x+1) = 6x+3\text{.}\) Write this under the last line. Again, make sure that like terms line up.
    \(x^2\) \(-\) \(4x\) \(+\) \(3\)
    \(2x\) \(+\) \(1\) \(2x^3\) \(-\) \(7x^2\) \(+\) \(2x\) \(+\) \(7\)
    \(-(2x^3\) \(+\) \(x^2)\)
    \(0\) \(-\) \(8x^2\) \(+\) \(2x\)
    \(-(-8x^2\) \(-\) \(4x)\)
    \(6x\) \(+\) \(7\)
    \(6x\) \(+\) \(3\)
    Step 10.
    Subtract \(6x+3\text{.}\)
    \(x^2\) \(-\) \(4x\) \(+\) \(3\)
    \(2x\) \(+\) \(1\) \(2x^3\) \(-\) \(7x^2\) \(+\) \(2x\) \(+\) \(7\)
    \(-(2x^3\) \(+\) \(x^2)\)
    \(0\) \(-\) \(8x^2\) \(+\) \(2x\)
    \(-(-8x^2\) \(-\) \(4x)\)
    \(6x\) \(+\) \(7\)
    \(-(6x\) \(+\) \(3)\)
    \(4\)
    Step 11.
    Now the degree of the 4 (on the last line) is lower than the degree of the divisor (\(2x+1\)). Therefore we stop here, and read off the answer. The answer is \(x^2-4x+3\text{,}\) with remainder 4. This means that:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x^2+5x+6}{x+3}=x+2 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \frac{2x^3-7x^2+2x+7}{2x+1} = x^2-4x+3 + \frac{4}{2x+1} \end{equation*}
    Quotient: \(x^2-4x+3\text{.}\)
    Remainder: 4

    Subsection 1.4.3 Additional Examples

    Here are a few additional examples.
    You are strongly encouraged to try the examples first on your own, before looking at the worked solutions.

    Example 1.28. Polynomial long division with "missing" terms.

    Use long division to divide the polynomial \(3x^4+2x^3+x^2+4\) by \(x^2+1\text{.}\) Find the quotient and the remainder (if there is one).
    Answer.
    \begin{equation*} \frac{3x^4+2x^3+x^2+4}{x^2+1} = 3x^2+2x-2 + \frac{-2x+6}{x^2+1} \end{equation*}
    Quotient: \(3x^2+2x-2\)
    Remainder: \(-2x+6\)
    Solution 1.
    Because there is no \(x\)-term, let’s insert \(0x\) as a placeholder. That will help keep things aligned, and will reduce the likelihood of mistakes. Let’s use placeholders for both the dividend, divisor and quotient whenever they are needed. Here is the worked out long division.
    \(x^2+0x+1\) \(3x^4\) \(+ \ \ \ 2x^3\) \(+\) \(x^2\) \(+\) \(0x\) \(+\) \(4\)
    Try solving the rest of the problem from here. Then when you are ready, click on ’Solution 2’ to see the rest of the solution.
    Solution 2.
    \(3x^2\) \(+ \ \ \ \ 2x\) \(-\) \(2\)
    \(x^2+0x+1\) \(3x^4\) \(+ \ \ \ 2x^3\) \(+\) \(x^2\) \(+\) \(0x\) \(+\) \(4\)
    \(-(3x^4\) \(+ \ \ \ 0x^3\) \(+\) \(3x^2)\)
    \(2x^3\) \(-\) \(2x^2\) \(+\) \(0x\)
