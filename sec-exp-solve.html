    Section 2.4 Solving with Exponentials and Logarithms

    Objectives

    • Solve exponential equations using a variety of strategies

      🔗
    • Solve logarithmic equations using a variety of strategies

      🔗

    Where will this be used in calculus?

    Solving exponential and logarithmic equations can be useful when looking for some specific value, for example:

    • Intersection points,

      🔗
    • Maximum/minimum points,
      🔗
      🔗
    • Points of inflection.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    With our review of fundamental characteristics of exponential and logarithmic functions under our belt, we will now review how to use these characteristics to algebraically solve equations involving exponential and logarithmic expressions. To do this, we will make use of the fact that exponential and logarithmic functions are inverses of each other, and thus "undo" each other’s work, much like radical functions undo the work of power functions. By the end of this section, we should be comfortable with algebraically solving equations involving exponential and logarithmic functions, and understand what these solutions would represent graphically.
    🔗

    Subsection 2.4.1 Section 1

    As we’ve established, logarithms and exponential functions can "cancel each other out" since they are inverses of each other. This is a similar relationship that a radical expression shares with a power function. Observe the familiar equation below:
    \begin{equation*} x^3=125 \end{equation*}
    Here, in order to solve for\(x\text{,}\) we would apply the inverse operation of a cube; the cube root:
    \begin{equation*} \sqrt[3]{x^3}=\sqrt[3]{125} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x=5 \end{equation*}
    A similar argument can be made to solve exponential and logarithmic functions. Consider the following exponential equation:
    \begin{equation*} e^x=100 \end{equation*}
    Just like with solving any other type of equation, here, we want to apply the inverse operation to solve for \(x\text{.}\) In the previous example, the inverse operation of a cube was a cube root. In this case, the inverse operation of the natural exponential function is the natural logarithm. Therefore, we will take \(\ln\) on both sides of the equation:

    Aside
    Study tip:
    🔗
    Remember that \(\ln\) is an operator, and has no actual value in and of itself. In this example, we would not say "multiply by \(\ln\) on both sides" as \(\ln\) has no numerical value on its own. The way to describe this step is to say that we "take" \(\ln\) on both sides. This is similar to how we would say "take the square root on both sides" rather than "multiply by square root on both sides.’’
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \ln(e^x)=\ln(100) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x=\ln(100)\approx4.605 \end{equation*}
